U.S. Military PCS Toolkit

PCS Move Checklist

A practical, offline-friendly checklist to guide each phase of your Permanent Change of Station (PCS) move. A PCS is a military move to a new duty station.

This checklist is organized to support military families first. The spouse and family checklist appears first, followed by tasks that must be completed by the service member.

Disclaimer: This checklist is for planning purposes only and does not replace official guidance from your unit, transportation office, or finance office.

Spouse & Family Checklist

These tasks focus on daily life, household planning, and family routines. They often run in parallel with the service member's official requirements.

What this is: Compare on-post housing, off-post neighborhoods, school districts, and commuting time. Why it matters: Housing availability and school options drive your timeline and help you choose the right neighborhood for your family.

Timing: Start 90–120 days out, or as soon as orders are in hand. Use the Military OneSource Plan My Move and DoDEA to compare schools.

  • Pitfall: Relying only on photos without verifying commute and school zoning.
  • Pro tip: Ask about temporary lodging allowance limits before signing a lease.

What this is: Line up child care coverage, school breaks, and family travel with confirmed move windows. Why it matters: A tight plan keeps kids safe and reduces last-minute leave changes.

Timing: 60–90 days before the move and update again when movers confirm dates. Use MilitaryChildCare.com for child care options.

  • Pitfall: Booking nonrefundable travel before the leave request is signed.
  • Pro tip: Ask the gaining unit about early reporting options for school start dates.

What this is: Create a room-by-room list of furniture, electronics, and valuable items before movers arrive. Why it matters: A solid inventory protects your claim and helps you spot missing boxes later.

Timing: 45–60 days out. Review Move.mil for household goods claims guidance.

  • Pitfall: Assuming the mover inventory is enough for claims.
  • Pro tip: Use short videos to capture working condition before pack-out.

What this is: Mark each room and clearly flag items that must stay with you. Why it matters: Labels speed up delivery day and prevent must-have items from being packed by mistake.

Timing: 1–2 weeks before pack-out. Reference the Move.mil preparing guide for do-not-pack rules.

  • Pitfall: Forgetting to label kids’ rooms, which delays setup.
  • Pro tip: Tape a printed floor plan to the front door on delivery day.

What this is: A carry-with-you bag with documents, medications, snacks, and chargers for travel and the first week. Why it matters: Household goods may arrive late, so you need essentials on hand.

Timing: "Pack 1–3 days before departure. Most PCS moves within the continental U.S. are by car, so prioritize comfort items (snacks, books, entertainment for kids) and clothing for overnight stops. If you're flying, Check the TSA “What Can I Bring?” list if you fly.

  • Pitfall: Packing passports or orders in checked luggage.
  • Pro tip: For driving trips, bring extra snacks and entertainment. For flights, use a clear folder so paperwork is easy at check-in.

What this is: Collect academic records, special education plans, and immunization documents and send them to the new school. Why it matters: Schools need records to enroll your child quickly.

Timing: 30–60 days before the move. Use Military Child Education Coalition resources for school transitions.

  • Pitfall: Waiting until arrival to request records, which delays enrollment.
  • Pro tip: Keep a sealed copy with you in case records lag behind.

What this is: Confirm pet policies, obtain health certificates, and plan pet-friendly travel. Why it matters: Airlines and states may require specific documentation and vaccines.

Timing: 45–60 days out. Check USDA APHIS pet travel requirements for your destination.

  • Pitfall: Waiting too long for international pet paperwork.
  • Pro tip: Freeze a water bowl so it melts during travel.

What this is: Forward your mail and update banks, insurance, medical providers, and subscriptions. Why it matters: Missing bills or medical notices adds stress in the first weeks.

Timing: 2–4 weeks before departure. Use the USPS Change of Address tool.

  • Pitfall: Forgetting to update online retailers for auto-deliveries.
  • Pro tip: Keep a simple spreadsheet to track who was updated.

What this is: Identify hotels, base lodging, or short-term rentals in case housing or deliveries are delayed. Why it matters: Temporary lodging is often needed between homes.

Timing: 30–60 days out. Review DoD Lodging availability and pet policies.

  • Pitfall: Booking outside of allowable lodging limits.
  • Pro tip: Keep receipts organized for reimbursement.

What this is: A simple plan for sleeping, meals, and routines while you wait for household goods. Why it matters: A calm first week helps kids adjust and reduces stress.

Timing: Finalize 1–2 weeks before departure. Use the Military OneSource moving hub for arrival tips.

  • Pitfall: Packing bedding in household goods boxes.
  • Pro tip: Use disposable plates for the first few days.

Service Member Checklist

These tasks must be handled by the service member because they involve official orders, finance, or command requirements. Either spouse can check items here so both can see progress.

What this is: Read the official orders for dates, destinations, and entitlements. Why it matters: Orders unlock movers, travel booking, and school/housing timelines.

Timing: Immediately once issued. Store a copy in a shared folder and use Move.mil for next steps.

  • Pitfall: Scheduling movers before orders are stamped as official.
  • Pro tip: Keep both digital and paper copies for check-ins.

What this is: Complete the relocation and transportation briefings required by your installation. Why it matters: Briefings unlock scheduling for shipments and travel entitlements.

Timing: Within 30 days of receiving orders. Check the Military OneSource moving page for workshop options.

  • Pitfall: Missing a briefing that blocks shipment scheduling.
  • Pro tip: Bring a notebook with questions about entitlements.

What this is: Verify your report date (RNLTD) and who is authorized to travel at government expense. Why it matters: This drives leave approvals, travel bookings, and child care timing.

Timing: 60–90 days out, then reconfirm 30 days before travel. See the Joint Travel Regulations overview for entitlements.

  • Pitfall: Assuming dependents are authorized without checking the orders.
  • Pro tip: Save a screenshot of any approved date change email.

What this is: Keep the Defense Enrollment Eligibility Reporting System (DEERS) and RAPIDS ID card system updated with correct dependents and addresses. Why it matters: Benefits, medical coverage, and ID cards depend on accurate records.

Timing: 30–60 days before moving, and again after arrival. Use the TRICARE DEERS page and RAPIDS Site Locator.

  • Pitfall: Not updating DEERS, leading to TRICARE enrollment issues.
  • Pro tip: Book RAPIDS appointments early; slots fill fast.

What this is: Review entitlements like Dislocation Allowance (DLA), per diem, and travel advances. Why it matters: Upfront funds help cover deposits, lodging, and travel costs.

Timing: 30–45 days before travel. Start with DFAS relocation pay guidance .

  • Pitfall: Forgetting that advances must be paid back if travel is canceled.
  • Pro tip: Keep a simple receipts folder by category.

What this is: Choose whether the government manages your household goods shipment or you do a Personally Procured Move (PPM), where you move yourself for reimbursement. Why it matters: The move type affects budgets, timelines, and how much work the family does.

Timing: 60–75 days before pack-out. Review Move.mil entitlements to compare options.

  • Pitfall: Choosing a PPM without help for heavy items.
  • Pro tip: Use the move organizer to estimate weight early.

What this is: Book pack-out and delivery windows with the office that manages military household goods shipments. Why it matters: Confirmed dates let the family plan travel and lodging.

Timing: 45–60 days before move. Use Move.mil to start the shipment request.

  • Pitfall: Waiting too long and losing your preferred dates.
  • Pro tip: Ask about weight limits so you can declutter early.

What this is: Finish all installation checkout requirements, return equipment, and collect required signatures. Why it matters: You cannot depart without a cleared checklist.

Timing: 30–45 days before departure. Use your installation out-processing checklist and the Military OneSource moving hub for reminders.

  • Pitfall: Waiting until the final week to clear supply or CIF.
  • Pro tip: Make a copy of your signed checklist for your records.

What this is: Transfer health records, schedule required screenings, and update TRICARE enrollment. Why it matters: It prevents gaps in care when you arrive.

Timing: 30–60 days before departure. Review TRICARE moving guidance.

  • Pitfall: Forgetting to update TRICARE region after arrival.
  • Pro tip: Request extra medication refills for travel delays.

What this is: Decide whether to ship, drive, or store your vehicle and prepare paperwork. Why it matters: Vehicle readiness affects travel safety and arrival timelines.

Timing: 45–60 days before travel. Use the Military OneSource vehicle shipping guide as a reference.

  • Pitfall: Leaving personal items in a vehicle before shipping.
  • Pro tip: Photograph the odometer and exterior before drop-off.

What this is: File the DD Form 1351-2 travel voucher and any additional claims for reimbursement. Why it matters: This closes out advances and pays back travel expenses.

Timing: Within 5–10 days of arrival. Use the DD Form 1351-2 and your finance office guidance.

  • Pitfall: Missing the submission deadline, which delays reimbursement.
  • Pro tip: Scan receipts during travel using a phone app.

What this is: Check in with the gaining unit, finish required orientation, and update local records. Why it matters: In-processing activates local access, housing, and medical services.

Timing: Within the first week of arrival. Review the Military OneSource after-the-move checklist for reminders.

  • Pitfall: Missing a required newcomer briefing and delaying access.
  • Pro tip: Ask for a local resource list for spouse employment and schools.

PCS Pitfalls and Pro Tips

  • Confirm medical and dental coverage transfers for the new location.
  • Ask early about special family needs screenings if applicable.
  • Review wills, powers of attorney, and family care plans if needed.
  • Check pay statements for changes after allowances update.
  • Keep digital copies of orders, receipts, and mover paperwork.
  • Share a calendar for mover dates, inspections, and school deadlines.
  • Take photos of high-value items before pack-out day.
